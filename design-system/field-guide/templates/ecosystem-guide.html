Temperate Rainforest

Cool, wet forests where precipitation and fog sustain year-round productivity

I

Identification

  • Epiphytes (moss, licorice fern) on branches indicate persistent moisture
  • Multi-layered canopy visible from a single viewpoint
  • Decaying logs support younger trees growing in a line
  • Limited mid-day sun on forest floor even in summer

Quick facts

Climate>140 cm annual precipitation; mild winters
Dominant treesSitka spruce, western hemlock, cedar
StructureEmergent · canopy · understory · forest floor
KeystoneFallen logs (nurse logs) seed new trees
RegionPacific Northwest coastal strip
DisturbanceWindthrow, fire (rare), logging history
II

Why it matters

Temperate rainforests store carbon, hold slope stability, and host species found nowhere else. Understanding structure helps you read any forest patch.

III

How it connects

  • Ocean fog supplements rainfall inland
  • Salmon nutrients link marine to terrestrial food webs
  • Soil mycorrhizae connect tree roots in shared networks
  • Climate change shifts precipitation timing and drought stress

VI

Outdoor investigation

  1. Stand in one spot for fifteen minutes. Count vertical layers you can see.
  2. Find a nurse log with seedlings — photograph and estimate log age from decay stage.
  3. Measure light at forest floor with a white card at noon.
  4. Return after a dry week: note moss color change on branches.
