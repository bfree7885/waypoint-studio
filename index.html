Mission

Observe · Understand · Create · Share

Technology is not the destination. Nature is. We build field laboratories — not feeds, not dashboards — so you can learn like an Earth Science class that never stops at the classroom door.

A family of destinations — each one a room in the same cabin. Every experience offers a place to learn, a gallery of evidence, field notes, seasonal news, curated videos, and tools that send you back outside.

Journal page · example
  • Write three lines after your next walk — weather, light, one surprise. No gear lists.
  • Sketch one leaf, one track, or one cloud formation. Labels can come later.
  • Honor empty days. A page that says nothing found is still science.
  • Share only when you choose. Your notes never become a public feed.

Outdoor challenge

One investigation at a time. Take a notebook. Leave the phone in your pocket unless you are documenting evidence.

This week · Late spring

The thirty-minute repeat

Find a spot you can return to — same tree, same fence post, same bend in the trail. Visit twice this week, thirty minutes apart or on different days.

  1. First visit: Note date, time, weather, three species you can identify, and one you cannot.
  2. Photograph from the same compass bearing — do not chase perfection; chase consistency.
  3. Second visit: What changed? Light, leaf-out, insects, sound? Write four sentences. No posting required.
  4. Reflect: What question would you ask an Earth Science class about this place?

Citizen science · private by default

You own your observations. Your photographs, field notes, and journals belong to you. The default experience is private — not a profile, not a feed, not a ranking.

When you choose to contribute to research or education, you will always understand what is collected, why it matters, and whether it stays private, anonymous, or public. Personal identity is never required. Location privacy is respected. You may opt out entirely.

Waypoint Studio will never prioritize likes, followers, engagement metrics, or algorithmic timelines. The goal is time outdoors — not attention online.
